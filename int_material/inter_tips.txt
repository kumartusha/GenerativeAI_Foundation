1. Know the Job Description

Understand the key skills, requirements, and responsibilities
Prepare examples that directly match what they’re looking for

2. Have a 30–45 Second Self-Introduction Ready

Who you are
What you do
Your top 2–3 achievements
Why you’re relevant for this role

3. Answer Clearly, Not Quickly

Speak at a steady pace
Avoid one-word or overly long answers
Use a structure: Situation → Action → Result

4. Highlight Your Top 3 Strengths

Connect them to the job
Keep them measurable (numbers, outcomes, impact)

5. Be Ready for the Basics Screening calls almost always cover:

Experience summary
Salary expectation
Notice period
Current location
Reason for job change
Joining timeline

6. Avoid Weak or Rambling Explanations Especially when discussing:

Career gaps
Job switches
Workplace conflicts Keep it professional, crisp, and factual.

7. Show Enthusiasm (Without Sounding Desperate) A confident, positive tone leaves a strong impression.

8. Keep Your Resume in Front of You

Keep it open on your screen
Highlight key points beforehand This prevents fumbling or forgetting details.

9. Ask 1–2 Smart Questions at the End Examples:

“What qualities do top performers in this role have?”
“What does success look like in the first 90 days?”

10. Follow Up Professionally A simple thank-you message boosts your overall impression. 

Screening Call Comparison: (Strong Candidate vs Average Candidate)
MOCK CALL 1 — The Strong, Clear Candidate
Recruiter:
Hi, this is Priya from ABC Corp. Is this a good time?

Candidate:
Yes, absolutely. Thank you for calling, Priya.

Recruiter:
Great. Could you start with a quick introduction about yourself?

Candidate (Strong 30–45 sec intro):
Sure. I’m Rohan, a Sales Executive with 3+ years of experience in BFSI. I currently work at XYZ Finance where I manage a portfolio of 120+ clients and have achieved 118% of my quarterly targets for the last two cycles. I specialise in lead conversion, relationship management, and cross-selling financial products. I’m now looking for a role where I can take on a larger portfolio and work in a more growth-oriented environment.

Recruiter:
Why are you looking for a change?

Candidate (Professional):
I’m looking for a role with stronger learning opportunities and clearer growth pathways. My current role has been great, but the scope for expansion is limited now.

Recruiter:
What is your notice period?

Candidate:
30 days. If required, I can negotiate for an early release.

Recruiter:
What are your salary expectations?

Candidate:
Based on my experience and current market standards, I’m looking at ₹8–9 LPA, but I’m flexible depending on the overall role and responsibilities.

Recruiter:
Any questions for me?

Candidate (Smart question):
Yes — what are the top qualities you expect in the person who will succeed in this role during the first 90 days?

 MOCK CALL 2 — Average Candidate (Needs Work)
Recruiter:
Tell me about yourself.

Candidate:
Uh… I’ve been working for 3 years… I do sales… I talk to clients and get business. I want growth now.

(Missing structure, no achievements, too vague.)

Recruiter:
Why are you leaving your current job?

Candidate:
There’s too much pressure and the manager is not supportive. (Negative, unprofessional, red flag.)

Recruiter:
Salary expectation?

Candidate:
As much as possible. (Shows no research or clarity.)

Recruiter:
Any questions?

Candidate:
No, nothing. (Shows lack of interest.) 

Key Takeaways
The difference between a good and an average screening call comes down to clarity, confidence, and preparation.
Small improvements in how you speak can completely change how recruiters perceive you.
A strong first impression isn’t luck — it’s built through preparation.
Screening calls reward candidates who communicate in a structured, professional, and precise manner.
Strong communication often beats strong experience — every time.
